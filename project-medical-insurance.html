What Really Drives Your Insurance Costs?

A data-driven exploration of the factors that determine medical insurance premiums

Tools: Python, Pandas, Tableau Dataset: 1,338 insurance records Focus: Exploratory Data Analysis

The Challenge

Most people don't understand why their insurance costs what it does. Is it age? Weight? Where you live? Without transparency, consumers can't make informed decisions about their health and finances.

Insurance pricing often feels like a black box. Multiple factors interact in complex ways, and the relative importance of each factor isn't always clear. This project aims to uncover the hidden patterns in medical insurance pricing using real-world data and clear visualization.

Key Findings

01

BMI is THE Dominant Cost Driver

Body Mass Index emerged as the single most significant factor in determining insurance costs. The dataset revealed that 52% of individuals fall into the "Obese" category, and these individuals face substantially higher medical expenses.

Median expenses increase significantly with BMI category

02

Age Matters, But Less Than Expected

While age does correlate with higher costs, the increase is gradual rather than exponential. The 56-64 age group shows interesting variance, likely due to the transition period before Medicare eligibility at 65.

Surprisingly, when controlled for BMI, age becomes a secondary factor. This suggests that lifestyle and health maintenance throughout life may be more important than chronological age alone.

03

Multiple Factors Create Complex Patterns

When visualizing age and BMI together, distinct expense clusters emerge. Younger individuals with normal BMI consistently show the lowest costs, while older individuals with higher BMI face the highest expenses.

This visualization reveals the multiplicative effect of risk factors‚Äîhaving multiple risk factors doesn't just add to costs, it amplifies them.

04

Geographic Location Shows Minimal Impact

Contrary to common assumptions about regional healthcare cost differences, all four regions (Northeast, Northwest, Southeast, Southwest) showed remarkably similar expense distributions. This suggests that individual health factors far outweigh geographic location in determining costs.

05

The Discount Eligibility Paradox

An unexpected finding: individuals WITH discount eligibility actually have higher average expenses than those without. This suggests that discount programs may be strategically targeted at high-risk, high-cost individuals‚Äîa smart business decision that helps manage the most expensive cases while maintaining customer satisfaction.

Impact & Takeaways

For Individuals

  • Weight management has the most significant impact on insurance costs
  • Age-based increases are gradual and manageable through healthy lifestyle choices
  • Geographic location shouldn't be a primary concern when shopping for coverage
  • Understanding your risk profile can help you make informed decisions about health and coverage

For Insurance Companies

  • BMI-based pricing is statistically justified and should be weighted heavily
  • Wellness programs targeting obesity prevention could significantly reduce overall costs
  • Regional pricing variations may be overvalued relative to other factors
  • Discount programs appear to be successfully targeting high-cost individuals

Limitations & Future Work

This analysis is based on a single dataset of 1,338 records and does not account for factors such as pre-existing conditions, specific medical procedures, or temporal trends in healthcare costs. Future analysis could incorporate:

  • Longitudinal data to track how costs change over time for individuals
  • Additional health metrics beyond BMI (blood pressure, cholesterol, etc.)
  • Socioeconomic factors that may influence both health outcomes and costs
  • Machine learning models to predict individual cost trajectories
